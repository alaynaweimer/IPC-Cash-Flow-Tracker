üéØ Why Cash Flow Matters

Cash is oxygen for your business. You can be profitable on paper and still go bankrupt if you run out of cash. Many businesses fail not because they're unprofitable, but because they can't pay bills when they're due.

Cash flow tells you the real story: Do you have enough money RIGHT NOW to cover your expenses? And how long will your current cash last?

üí∞ The #1 rule: Cash in must exceed cash out. Sounds obvious, but timing differences kill businesses. A client paying 60 days late while your rent is due NOW is a cash flow crisis ‚Äî even if you're "profitable."

üìê Cash Flow Basics

üíµ Cash Inflows

Money coming INTO your business: client payments, sales, loans, investments, refunds, interest income.

üí∏ Cash Outflows

Money going OUT of your business: rent, payroll, software, supplies, taxes, loan payments, owner draws.

üìä Net Cash Flow

The difference: Inflows ‚àí Outflows. Positive = you're building cash. Negative = you're burning cash.

üéØ The Goal: 6-Month Runway

Most financial advisors recommend keeping 3-6 months of expenses in reserve. For small businesses with irregular income, aim for the higher end. This buffer lets you survive a bad quarter, invest in growth, and sleep at night.

üíº Pro Tips From the Field

  • Track cash flow weekly, not monthly. Monthly is too slow. A weekly 15-minute check catches problems before they become crises.
  • Get paid faster. Invoice immediately. Offer 2% discount for payment within 10 days. Use autopay for retainers.
  • Delay outflows (ethically). Pay bills on the due date, not before. Use credit cards for the float (but pay in full).
  • Build a cash reserve before you need it. Set aside 10% of every payment until you have 6 months of runway.
  • Know your "zero day." If all income stopped today, when would you run out of cash? That's your wake-up number.
  • Separate business and personal. Mixing accounts makes cash flow invisible. Keep them separate.
